COGNITIVE TESTING RESULTS AND ANALYSIS

Cognition generally refers to the brain processes through which we discover and understand the world around us, and how we apply that knowledge from day to day. These processes include making sense of perceptions, storing and retrieving memories, learning, forming concepts, solving problems, planning activities, utilizing language, achieving insights and abstract thinking. To measure Madison’s cognitive ability, she was administered 10 subtests from the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children-Fifth Edition (WISC-V). The WISC-V is an individually administered, comprehensive clinical instrument for assessing the intelligence of children ages 6:0-16:11. Subtest scores range from 1 to 19, with scores between 8 and 12 typically considered average. The subtest scores contribute to the index scores, which represent intellectual functioning in five cognitive areas: Verbal Comprehension Index (VCI), Visual Spatial Index (VSI), Fluid Reasoning Index (FRI), Working Memory Index (WMI), and the Processing Speed Index (PSI).

This assessment also produces a Full Scale IQ (FSIQ) composite score that represents general intellectual ability. The primary index scores and the FSIQ are on a standard score metric with a mean of 100 and an SD of 15. The primary index scores range from 45 to 155; the FSIQ ranges from 40 to 160. For both the primary index scores and the FSIQ, scores ranging from 90 to 109 are typically considered average.

A percentile rank (PR) is provided for each reported composite and subtest score to show Madison’s standing relative to other same-age children in the WISC-V normative sample. If the percentile rank for her Verbal Comprehension Index score is 77, for example, it means that she performed as well as or better than approximately 77% of children her age. It is common for children to exhibit score differences across areas of performance. It is possible for intellectual abilities to change over the course of childhood. Additionally, a child’s scores on the WISC-V can be influenced by motivation, attention, interests, and opportunities for learning. All scores may be slightly higher or lower if Madison were tested again on a different day. It is therefore important to view these test scores as a snapshot of Madison’s current level of intellectual functioning. When these scores are used as part of a comprehensive evaluation, they contribute to an understanding of her current strengths and any needs that can be addressed. Also, with experience and opportunity for learning as she develops, Madison is likely to improve her logical thinking and reasoning skills.

The Similarities subtest is designed to measure verbal concept formation and abstract reasoning with verbal information. It involves the ability to make associations and form categories with language. Students who perform well are likely to be fluid and thoughtful with how they analyze verbal information, making connections in order to move along when processing language. Madison’s overall score was witin the average range. Tasks in this index involve constructing designs and puzzles under a time constraint.

Fluid Reasoning

The Fluid Reasoning Index (FRI) represents Madison’s overall ability to engage in fluid reasoning which is the capacity to think logically and solve problems in novel (new) situations, independent of acquired knowledge. Fluid reasoning is necessary for logical problem solving such as needed for science, math and technical problem solving as well as learning new procedures quickly. It generally involves visual intelligence, simultaneous processing, and abstract thinking. Her overall score on the FRI fell in the High Average range.

The Fluid Reasoning Index score is an average score derived from the Matrix Reasoning and Figure Weights subtests. Using reasoning processes involves recognizing patterns and deducing information. The Matrix Reasoning subtest is designed to measure fluid and non-verbal intelligence and utilized Madison’s spatial and simultaneous processing ability (ability to process multiple pieces of information at the same time). All told, it represents her ability to deduce other things based on information presented. The subtest required Madison to recognize patterns in order to make predictions. Madison scored fairly high on this subtest. Scores can be an indicator of how Madison can make sense of the world around her. For the Figure Weights subtest, Madison viewed drawings of scales with missing weights and selected the response options that kept the scales balanced. The Figure Weights subtest examines one’s ability to match information utilizing quantitative reasoning (such as doubling) and inductive reasoning together. She also scored well on this novel task and better than her typical peers. Her pattern of scores suggests that inductive reasoning is similarly developed relative to quantitative reasoning.

Working Memory

The Working Memory Index (WMI) measured Madison’s ability to register, maintain, and manipulate visual and auditory information mentally, which requires memory, attention and concentration. Working memory is a system for temporarily storing information in one’s short-term memory and managing and manipulating the information to perform an operation. Her overall score on the WMI fell in the high end of the average range.

The working memory Index is made up of two subtests, Digit Span and Picture Span. For Digit Span, Madison was read numerical sequences and needed to repeat the sequence either forward, backward or in ascending order. The Digit Span Forward task measures auditory rehearsal (repeating information mentally to support memory) and short-term memory. It can reflect how long Madison is able to hold onto what she hears and repeat it back. Examples for uses of one’s short-term memory involve holding onto an instruction, a phone number for example, or any information presented orally. She scored solidly (DSf=10) and was able to remember numerical sequences up to 7 numbers long. On Digit Span Backward, Madison was read a string of numbers and asked to repeat them in backwards order. This task involves working memory, mental manipulation of information and sometimes involves visuospatial imaging. Working memory plays a key role in making calculations mentally or formulating a plan in one’s head. Among other things, working memory impacts learning to read and perform math. Madison scored well on this task (DSb=13). Working memory and short-term memory are two distinctive parts of the brain that work together, and Madison was well-developed in both areas.

On the Sequencing task of the Digit Span subtest, Madison needed to remember and order the numbers in numerical order. Some students do well with this task because it provides a strategy to organize the information and performance suggests ability with either sequencing information and/or utilizing a strategy to order information. Using strategies or ordering information are important skills when organizing a plan or procedure. Madison also scored well on this task. She obtained a good score for a student her age (DSs=11), and her pattern of scores suggests she orders information appropriately when given a strategy to do so.

On the Picture Span subtest, Madison viewed rows of objects and was required to recount them in the same order when the page was turned. Students who label the object with words mentally often perform better and can utilize their verbal memory to support their visual memory. Madison scored well and within the high end of the average range on this task. She was able to sequence objects she saw on this task with support of her verbal working memory fairly well. Scores indicate that visual as well as verbal instruction are both successful means of teaching Madison and that use of visuals supports her learning.

Social Stress—Feelings of stress and tension in personal relationships; a feeling of being excluded from social activities

Somatization—The tendency to be overly sensitive to, to experience, or to complain about relatively minor physical problems and discomforts

Withdrawal—The tendency to evade others to avoid social contact

Adaptive Scale Descriptions

Adaptability—The ability to adapt readily to changes in the environment

Functional Communication—The ability to express ideas and communicate in a way others can easily understand

Interpersonal Relations— The perception of having good social relationships and friendships with peers

Leadership—The skills associated with accomplishing academic, social, or community goals, including the ability to work with others

Relations With Parents—A positive regard toward parents and a feeling of being esteemed by them

Self-Esteem—Feelings of self-esteem, self-respect, and self-acceptance

Self-Reliance—Confidence in one’s ability to solve problems; a belief in one’s personal dependability and decisiveness

Social Skills—The skills necessary for interacting successfully with peers and adults in home, school, and community settings

Study Skills—The skills that are conducive to strong academic performance, including organizational skills and good study habits
